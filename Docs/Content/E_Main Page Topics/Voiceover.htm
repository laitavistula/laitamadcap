Voice-over

Voice-over involves the simultaneous broadcast of the original soundtrack and its translation. Initially, the original audio is fully audible, but its volume is gradually reduced as the translated version becomes more prominent, continuing this way until the end.

This mode of multimedia translation creates a realistic effect, making it particularly suitable for documentaries and interviews. Voice-over is often regarded as a "cost-effective alternative to dubbing," which explains its widespread use in former Communist states, as well as in parts of the Middle East and Asia.

Although voice-over is the least popular of the three main modes of audiovisual translation (AVT), its use remains prominent in former Eastern Bloc countries, such as Poland and Russia. This method typically features a single narrator—often male—reading all roles, including those of women and children.

Voice-over is favored by many viewers for its accessible format, as it eliminates the need to read subtitles or engage in additional intellectual effort; listeners simply follow the narrator's voice. In Poland, it is the most commonly used form of AVT on television. According to a 2002 survey, 50.2% of Poles preferred voice-over over other translation modes.
