Dubbing

Dubbing, sometimes referred to as "lip-synchrony," involves translating dialogue, synchronizing it with the original audio, and re-recording actors' performances. It is considered one of the most comprehensive forms of audiovisual translation. The process adheres closely to the timing, phrasing, and lip movements of the original dialogue to maintain natural synchronization. While typically interlingual, there are rare cases of intralingual dubbing.

Types of Dubbing:

  • AI Dubbing

AI-powered dubbing tools are revolutionizing the process by translating spoken dialogue and generating voiceovers in different languages. Using text-to-speech (TTS) technology, these tools create synthetic voices that match the translated script.

Emerging AI systems can even synchronize lip movements with the dubbed audio, producing a seamless and natural result. While still evolving, these technologies are increasingly focused on improving the fluidity and expressiveness of voiceovers.

Example: Platforms like Synthesia enable video creators to generate multilingual voiceovers quickly, using AI-generated avatars or synthetic voices.

  • Partial dubbing

Also known as "half-dubbing" or "concise synchronization," partial dubbing adds pre-recorded spoken text to the original soundtrack. Unlike full dubbing, it doesn’t translate every element of the audio but provides key information in the target language.

AVT Modes Across Europe

Frederic Chaume outlined a "map" of the European AVT landscape, categorizing countries based on their preferred modes of audiovisual translation:

  • Dubbing Countries

Austria, France, Germany, Hungary, Italy, Czech Republic, Slovakia, Spain, Turkey, Ukraine

  • Subtitling Countries

Albania, Croatia, Cyprus, Denmark, Finland, Greece, Iceland, Ireland, the Netherlands, Norway, Portugal, Romania, Serbia, Slovenia, Sweden, United Kingdom, and many more

  • Voice-Over Countries

Poland (for TV), Russia, Bulgaria, Latvia, Lithuania

  • Countries Using Both Dubbing and Subtitling

    • Belgium: Dubbing in Wallonia, subtitling in Flanders

    • Bulgaria: TV series are dubbed, films are usually subtitled, and some programs are voiced-over by at least four actors
